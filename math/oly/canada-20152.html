An Inequality on the Product of Altitude Components

Let \(ABC\) be an acute-angled triangle with altitudes \(AD\), \(BE\), and \(CF\). Let \(H\) be the orthocentre, that is the point where the altitudes meet. Prove that \[\frac{AB\cdot AC + BC\cdot BA + CA\cdot CB}{AH\cdot AD + BH\cdot BE + CH\cdot CF} \leq 2.\]

Solution

I should come back to clean up this solution because it's embarrassingly long... But nonetheless, here is a solution:

Let \(a\), \(b\), \(c\) denote the lengths of the sides \(BC\), \(AC\), and \(AB\) respectively. We begin with an obvious statement of AM-GM: \[ab + bc + ca \leq a^2 + b^2 + c^2\] is equivalent to \[\begin{align*} \frac{1}{a} + \frac{1}{b} + \frac{1}{c} &\leq \frac{a^2 + b^2 + c^2}{abc} \\ &= \frac{\left(c^2 + b^2 - a^2\right) + \left(a^2 + c^2 - b^2\right) + \left(a^2 + b^2 - c^2\right)}{abc} \\ &= 2\left(\frac{\cos A}{a} + \frac{\cos B}{b} + \frac{\cos C}{c}\right). \end{align*}\]

Now suppose \(x = c\cdot AF\), \(y = a\cdot BD\), and \(z = b\cdot CE\). We claim that \[\begin{align*} x\cos B\cos C &= \frac{xyz}{a^2bc},\\ y\cos C\cos A &= \frac{xyz}{ab^2c}, \\ z\cos A\cos B &= \frac{xyz}{abc^2}. \end{align*}\]

By the symmetry of the configuration, WLOG it suffices to show that \(x\cos B\cos C = \frac{xyz}{a^2bc}\). We have \[\begin{align*} x\cos B\cos C &= c\cdot AF\cdot\cos B\cos C \\ &= (c\cos B)\cdot AF\cdot\cos C \\ &= BD\cdot AF\cdot \frac{CE}{a} \\ &= \frac{xyz}{a^2bc}. \end{align*}\]

Multiplying both sides of our derived inequality by \(\frac{xyz}{abc}\), we get \[\begin{align*} &\quad \sum_{cyc}\frac{xyz}{a^2bc} \leq 2\sum_{cyc}\left(\frac{xyz}{a^2bc}\cos A\right)\\ \iff&\quad \sum_{cyc}x\cos B\cos C \leq 2(x+y+z)\cos A\cos B\cos C\\ \iff&\quad \frac{x}{\cos A} + \frac{y}{\cos B} + \frac{z}{\cos C} \leq 2(x+y+z), \end{align*}\]

where the cyclic sum is taken across the triplet \((a\), \(b\), \(c)\), the triplet \((x\), \(y\), \(z)\), and the triplet \((A\), \(B\), \(C)\). Also note that \(0 < \cos A\), \(\cos B\), \(\cos C < 1\) since \(ABC\) is acute. Now observe that \[ \frac{x} {\cos A} = \frac{c\cdot AF}{\cos A} = \frac{c\cdot AF\cdot b}{AF} = bc,\] as well as \(\frac{y}{\cos B}=ab\) and \(\frac{z}{\cos C} = ca\). Furthermore, it is well known that the points \(F\), \(B\), \(D\), \(H\), the points \(H\), \(D\), \(C\), \(E\), and the points \(E\), \(A\), \(F\), \(H\) are respectively concyclic, so by the power of a point,

\[\begin{align*} x &= c\cdot AF = AH\cdot AD, \\ y &= a\cdot BD = BH\cdot BE, \\ z &= b\cdot CE = CH\cdot CF. \end{align*}\]

Substituting for the inequality above gives us \[ \frac{AB\cdot AC + BC\cdot BA + CA\cdot CB}{AH\cdot AD + BH\cdot BE + CH\cdot CF} \leq 2.\]

\(\blacksquare\)

Motivation

The motivation for the solution is to think about how to interpret each product that appears in the expression on the left-hand side. Initially, the products \(AB\cdot AC\), \(BC\cdot BA\), and \(CA\cdot CB\) motivate us to think about area, but this line of thought is shot down by the fact that \(\sin\) doesn't give us the lengths we care about. The lengths we care about are the ones that appear in the problem statement as well as the ones that can be derived from power of a point.

With this train of thought however, one might reach the critical insight, which is that \[AB\cdot AC\cdot\cos A = AB\cdot AC\cdot \frac{AE}{AB} = AE\cdot AC = AH\cdot AD,\] where the same relation holds for the other angles. A natural train of thought is: if \(\sin\) didn't work, why not \(\cos\)? Or one can realize that \(AE\) is the projection of \(AB\) onto side \(AC\). Or instead of thinking about areas, think about dot products. Any one of these should lead to this key insight.

From here, the problem becomes substantially easier, so the rest is mostly algebraic and geometric grunt work.
