Maximally Parasocial

At a social gathering of introverts, it was found that for each pair of people, exactly one of them knew the other. Prove that there was someone in the gathering that each person either knew or knew someone else who did.

Solution

Call the person that was known to the most number of people \(A\). We claim that person \(A\) is the one with the desired properties.

Suppose the number of people who knew \(A\) was \(k\). If \(A\) did not know anyone in the gathering, then everyone must have known \(A\) so our work is done. Otherwise, \(A\) must have known someone else, say person \(B\). It then suffices to show that \(B\) knew someone that knew \(A\).

If \(B\) did not know anyone else who knew \(A\), then clearly, \(B\) must not have known the \(k\) people who knew \(A\). Thus, \(B\) must have been known to the \(k\) people who knew \(A\). And since \(A\) knew \(B\), in total, \(B\) must have been known by \(k+1\) people. This contradicts the maximality of the number of people who knew \(A\), so our assumption that \(B\) did not know anyone who knew \(A\) is false. \(B\) must have known someone who knew \(A\).

\(\blacksquare\)

Motivation

This is a prototypical example of an existence problem where we discover the construction through the extremal principle. We hypothesize that the desired object maximizes some function, and if said hypothesis is in fact true, the solution should come out via assuming falsity and arriving at some contradiction.
