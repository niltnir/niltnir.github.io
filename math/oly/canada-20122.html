LCM of Common Numbers

For any positive integers \(n\) and \(k\), let \(L(n,k)\) be the least common multiple of the \(k\) consecutive integers \(n\), \(n+1\), \(\dots\), \(n+k-1\). Show that for any integer \(b\), there exist integers \(n\) and \(k\) such that \(L(n, k) > bL(n + 1,k)\).

Solution

\(\gdef\lcm{\operatorname{lcm}}\)

Given any positive integer \(b\), let \(p>b\) be an odd prime number. We claim that \(n=p^2\), \(k=2p\) satisfies the condition.

Let \(C\) be the least common multiple of all the integers \(p^2+t\) where \(t\) ranges from 1 and \(2p-1\) inclusive. Since \(p^2\nmid p^2+t\) for each \(t\), we have \[L(n,k) = L(p^2, 2p) = \lcm(p^2, C) = pC.\] Furthermore, \[L(n+1, k) = L(p^2+1, 2p) = \lcm(p^2+2p, C) = C.\] The last line follows from the fact that \(p^2+2p=p(p+2)\), where both \(p\) and \(p+2\) are within the range of \(t\). In other words, there must exist multiples of \(p\) and \(p+2\) among the \(2p-1\) consecutive integers that produce \(C\).

As such, we conclude that \[L(n,k) = pC = pL(n+1,k) > bL(n+1, k).\]

\(\blacksquare\)

Motivation

The motivation for the solution is to notice that \(L(n,k)\) and \(L(n + 1, k)\) doesn't change "by much". This is in the sense that the least common multiples that these expressions are evaluating basically the same thing except the first or last term in a sequence of consecutive integers. This motivates us to invent \(C\), the least common multiple of the "common" numbers between each sequence.

The rest of the solution stems from trying to control the behavior of \(C\) to not take too many or too few factors given arbitrary \(n\) and \(k\). This is a construction problem, so once an example is found, we're done.
