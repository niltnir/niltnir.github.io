Divisibility of 2n Numbers

Among \(n + 1\) positive integers \(\leq 2n\), prove that there are two which are relatively prime and two where one divides the other.

Solution

We first show that there exist two relatively prime numbers.

For each positive integer \(k \leq 2n\), \(\gcd(k, k+1) = 1\). Pairing each odd \(k\) with \(k+1\), we get \(n\) pairs among which \(n + 1\) numbers are chosen. By the pigeonhole principle, there must exist a pair where both numbers are chosen.

Next, we show that there exist two numbers where one divides the other.

Define an \(\textit{odd root}\) of a positive integer \(k\) as the odd positive integer \(r\) such that \(k = 2^t\cdot r\) for some non-negative integer \(t\). We partition the positive integers from \(1\) to \(2n\) based on their odd root. Since there are \(n\) odd positive integers, clearly there will be \(n\) partitions.

We now claim that any two numbers in the same partition have the property that one divides the other. Suppose WLOG that positive integers \(a\) and \(b\) have the same odd root \(r_0\) and \(a > b\). We can write \(a\) as \(2^{a_0}\cdot r_0\) and \(b\) as \(2^{b_0} \cdot r_0\) for some non-negative integers \(a_0\) and \(b_0\). Since \(a > b\), clearly, \(a_0 > b_0\), so \[a = 2^{a_0}\cdot r_0 = 2^{a_0 - b_0} \cdot 2^{b_0} \cdot r_0 = 2^{a_0 - b_0}\cdot b.\]

Among the \(n\) partitions, \(n+1\) numbers are chosen, so by the pigeonhole principle, there must exist a partition in which two numbers are chosen.

\(\blacksquare\)
