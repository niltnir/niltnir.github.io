The Mean-Map of a Sequence

Let the \(\textit{mean-map}\) of a finite sequence of real numbers be a new sequence where each term in the original sequence is replaced by the arithmetic mean of the term and the next term after it (where the last term is paired with the first term). If we start with a sequence of integers where no two terms are equal, then prove that iteratively taking the mean-map of the seqeunce will eventually produce non-integral terms.

Solution

We will first prove the following assertion.

If \(q_1\), \(q_2\), \(\dots\), \(q_n\) \((n > 2)\) is a seqeunce of pairwise distinct integers, then no matter how many times the mean-map is taken, a seqeuence will never have all equal terms.

Assume FTSOC that at some point all the terms become equal. Suppose the sequence \(r_1\), \(r_2\), \(\dots\), \(r_n\) of integers are not all equal and the map \[ \left(r_1, r_2, \dots, r_n\right) \mapsto \left(\frac{r_1+r_2} {2}, \frac{r_2+r_3} {2}, \dots, \frac{r_n+r_1} {2}\right) \] yields \[\frac{r_1+r_2}{2} = \frac{r_2+r_3}{2} = \dots = \frac{r_n+r_1}{2} \iff r_1+r_2 = r_2+r_3 = \dots = r_n+r_1.\]

If \(n\) is odd, then we derive that \(r_1 = r_2 = \dots = r_n\), contradicting the fact that they are not all equal. It suffices to then consider the case when \(n\) is even.

If \(n\) is even, the above equality yields \(r_1 = r_3 = \dots = r_{n-1}\) and \(r_2 = r_4 = \dots = r_n\). Suppose that \(x = r_1\) and \(y = r_2\). If \(x=y\), then we get a contradiction as in the odd case, so consider \(x\neq y\). It suffices to show that there cannot exist a sequence of integers \(s_1\), \(s_2\), \(\dots\), \(s_n\) such that \[ \left(s_1, s_2, \dots, s_n\right) \mapsto \left(x, y, \dots, x, y\right).\] If such a sequence exists, then \(s_1 + s_2 = s_3 + s_4 = \dots = s_{n-1} + s_n = 2x\) and \(s_2 + s_3 = s_4 + s_5 = \dots = s_n + s_1 = 2y\). From which it follows that \(\sum_{i=1}^n s_i = nx\) and \(\sum_{i=1}^n s_i = ny\) respectively. However, since \(x \neq y\), this is clearly a contradiction.

Lastly, we show that the sum of the pairwise distances between terms always decreases. Let \(D_k\) be the sum after taking \(k\) mean-maps, we have \[\begin{align*} D_k &= \sum_{1\leq i\leq j\leq n} \left| q_i - q_j \right|\\ &= 2\sum_{1\leq i\leq j\leq n} \frac{\left| q_i - q_j \right|}{2} \\ &= \sum_{1\leq i\leq j\leq n} \frac{\left| q_i - q_j \right| + \left| q_{i+1} - q_{j+1} \right|}{2} \\ &> \sum_{1\leq i\leq j\leq n} \left|\frac{q_i + q_{i+1}}{2} - \frac{q_j + q_{j+1}}{2}\right| \\ &= D_{k+1}, \end{align*}\] where indicies are taken cyclically modulo \(n\).

Since \(D_k \geq 0\) always decreases after taking a mean-map and the terms never equate to one another (\(D_k \neq 0\) for each \(k\)), eventually \(D_k\) will stop decreasing by integers, at which point it will decrease by a non-integer. The distance between terms will then be non-integral, so some term must become non-integral.

\(\blacksquare\)

Motivation

There are two key insights to the solution. The first insight is to come up with the monovariant \(D_k\): the sum of the pairwise distances between terms. Enough experimentation should reveal that the terms seem to "converge" together after enough mean-maps. This then motivates the next key insight which is to question whether all the terms become equal. This is important because the equality case is a fixed point under a mean-map. To guarantee that we always get non-integral terms, we must eliminate the potentially unwieldly cases where the configurations somehow jumps to this fixed point. Once these two insights are found however, the rest are minor details.
