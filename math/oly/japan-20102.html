Powers of 2 Divide \(n^n - m\)

Let \(k\) and \(m\) be positive integers such that \(m\) is odd. Prove that there exists some positive integer \(n\) such that \(n^n - m\) is divisible by \(2^k\).

Solution

We prove the claim by induction on \(k\). For the base case when \(k=1\), any odd \(n\) will suffice. Now suppose that for some \(k\), there exists \(n=N\) such that \(2^k\mid n^n-m = N^N-m\) for each odd \(m\). Note that \(N\) must be odd. We then show that there exists some \(n\) such that \(2^{k+1}\mid n^n-m\) for each odd \(m\).

If \(2^{k+1}\mid N^N-m\), we are done by letting \(n=N\). On the other hand, suppose \(2^{k+1}\nmid N^N-m\). By assumption, \(2^k\mid N^N-m\) so let \(N^N-m=2^k\cdot t\) for odd positive integer \(t\).

We claim that \(2^{k+1}\) divides \[\left(2^k + N\right)^{\left(2^k + N\right)} - m.\]

Indeed since both \(N\) and \(t\) are odd,

\[\begin{align*} \left(2^k + N\right)^{\left(2^k + N\right)} - m &\equiv \left(2^k + N\right)^N - m\pmod{2^{k+1}}\\ &\equiv N^N + \tbinom{N}{1}N^{N-1}\cdot 2^k - m \pmod{2^{k+1}}\\ &= 2^k\left(t + N^N\right) \\ &\equiv 0 \pmod{2^{k+1}}, \end{align*}\]

as desired.

\(\blacksquare\)

Motivation

The motivation for the solution is to analyze the quantity \(n^n\) modulo \(2^k\) for each positive integer \(k\) and odd \(n\).

For \(k=1\), odd \(n^n\) yields \(1\), \(3\) \(\pmod{4}\).

For \(k=2\), odd \(n^n\) yields \(1\), \(3\), \(5\), \(7\) \(\pmod{8}\).

For \(k=3\), odd \(n^n\) yields \(1\), \(11\), \(5\), \(7\), \(9\), \(3\), \(13\), \(15\) \(\pmod{16}\).

For \(k=4\), odd \(n^n\) yields \(1\), \(27\), \(21\), \(23\), \(9\), \(19\), \(29\), \(15\), \(17\), \(11\), \(5\), \(7\), \(25\), \(3\), \(13\), \(31\) \(\pmod{32}\).

To reach the solution above, it suffices to notice that for each term in the \(k=k_0\) sequence, we either add \(2^{k_0}\) or leave it be to reach the \(k=k_0+1\) sequence (looping through the \(k=k_0\) sequence once).

Interestingly, the problem statement is also equivalent to saying that these quantities are unique modulo \(2^k\) (ie. there does not exist \(n_0\) and \(n_1\) such that \(n_0^{n_0} \equiv n_1^{n_1}\).)

With that mindset, one of the difficulties was in avoiding a certain anti-pattern/red herring. For each \(k\) from \(1\) to \(4\), we mysteriously find that if \(n_0^{n_0} \equiv n_1 \pmod{2^k}\), then \(n_1^{n_1} \equiv n_0 \pmod{2^k} \).

When I was solving the problem, I ended up trying to prove this anti-pattern, which actually stops at \(k=5\). For example, \(5^5 \equiv 53 \pmod{64}\) but \(53^{53} \equiv 37 \not\equiv 5 \pmod{64}\).
