Shooting Lemma on an Angle Equality

Let \(\Gamma\) be the circumcircle of triangle \(ABC\). A circle with center \(O\) is tangent to side \(BC\) at point \(P\) and tangent to the arc \(BC\) of \(\Gamma\) not containing \(A\) at point \(Q\). Prove that if \(\angle{BAO} = \angle{CAO}\), then \(\angle{PAO} = \angle{QAO}\).

Solution

Let \(M\) be the midpoint of arc \(BC\) containing \(A\), \(N \neq A\) be the intersection of line \(AO\) with \(\Gamma\), \(O_{\Gamma}\) be the center of \(\Gamma\), and \(\omega\) be the circle with center \(O\) described in the problem statement.

\(M\), \(P\), and \(Q\) are then collinear by the so called \(\textit{shooting lemma}\), which we prove as follows. It is clear that there exists a homothety mapping \(\omega\) to \(\Gamma\), since \(O_\Gamma\), \(O\), and \(Q\) are collinear, \(MO_{\Gamma} \) is parallel to \(PO\) (both perpendicular to \(\overline{BC})\), and both \(\triangle{MO_{\Gamma}Q}\) and \(\triangle{POQ}\) are isosceles. Consequently, \(M\), \(P\), and \(Q\) are collinear.

We now claim that \(M\), \(O_{\Gamma}\), \(N\) are also collinear. We have \[ \measuredangle CBN = \measuredangle CAN = \measuredangle CAO = \measuredangle OAB = \measuredangle NAB = \measuredangle NCB, \] so \(N\) must be the midpoint of arc \(\widehat{BC}\) not containing \(A\). Thus, \(MN\) is simply the diameter of \(\Gamma\).

We now use the above to show that \(A\), \(P\), \(O\), \(Q\) are concyclic. Using the homothety defined above, we see that \[ \measuredangle OPQ = \measuredangle O_{\Gamma}MQ = \measuredangle NMQ = \measuredangle NAQ = \measuredangle OAQ, \] so \(A\), \(P\), \(O\), \(Q\) are concyclic.

Lastly, since \(\triangle{OPQ}\) is isosceles, \[ \measuredangle QAO = \measuredangle QPO = \measuredangle OQP = \measuredangle OAP. \]

\(\blacksquare\)

Motivation

Once the configuration for the shooting lemma is found, the key observation is that \(A\), \(P\), \(O\), and \(Q\) are concyclic. This requires discovering point \(N\) along the way, which is both the antipode of \(M\) and the intersection of \(AO\) with \(\Gamma\).
